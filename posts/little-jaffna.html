Film September 2025

Little Jaffna

Lawrence Valin's debut gangster film — about rival Sri Lankan Tamil gangs in Paris — is the rare movie that earns the word masterpiece without breaking a sweat.

It is hard to believe this is a debut feature film, even harder to believe the director both directed and acted in it. The movie knows exactly what it wants, is intentional in every frame, and is not afraid to show it.

Show, don't tell. Every character is carefully crafted — whether it is Vela Ramamurthy playing the South Asian godfather (Aya) who is utterly believable (unlike the bombastic villains we are used to seeing in South Asian crime dramas), or Lawrence as the protagonist navigating his two identities as an undercover cop, or the cast who are predominantly new actors and bring a natural flair to their roles.

The film doesn't feel the need to make every character a testament to the South Asian diaspora in the global eye — it gives them the freedom of variety and creativity. Do you ever watch Hollywood superhero movies and think "all Americans fly"? So why do we worry if audiences will think all South Asians might be gangsters?

Everything is carefully curated so that the movie focuses on the story while the cultural subtext just exists. It exists, it is relevant, and it is authentic. This is a big difference from the recent stream of movies, many of which fail to focus enough on the story while getting bogged down by cultural context or social messaging.

The devil is in the details. There are subtle choices throughout that make the (sub)conscious storytelling all the more effective: the protagonist's vitiligo on his face as a metaphor for his dual identity; eating with a fork as a proxy for belonging, morphing throughout the movie; the film treating the Sri Lankan civil war like it happened yesterday by adding chronologically incongruous events (like Vijay's Theri release). Bonding over food, celebrating new movies, the dichotomy of individualism vs. belonging, struggling with the conformity that comes with belonging — all classic South Asian experiences shown beautifully on screen.

What makes a masterpiece? I think there are three main characteristics: an engaging story, technical excellence, and cultural significance. You don't need all three for an excellent movie — but when the three come together, it is an undeniable masterpiece. Little Jaffna deftly handles all three.

A new wave of South Asian stories. This is my second movie this year after Santosh (2024) that has a tight script with compelling story and nuanced characters, is about South Asian characters directed by someone of South Asian origin, but with western technical crew and thus western sensibilities in pacing, storytelling, and aesthetics. Santosh sticks to the storytelling rather than exploring identity or cultural nuance. Little Jaffna does this willfully and masterfully. Whatever their creative differences, both are bold, refreshing, and entertaining — filling the void left by the creative bankruptcy of Hollywood.

There are some specific details from the Director's Q&A I am trying to avoid so that the movie remains about the movie itself and not about the director — who is very articulate about his craft and motivations. Nevertheless, this is an excellent movie that will pave the way for many more creative, bold voices to follow: ones that reflect the new globalized world we live in.

Verdict

Between Hollywood creative bankruptcy and South Asian cinema that struggles to make a full break from its regressive past, Little Jaffna gives incredible hope.
