Beef, Season 2

Lee Sung Jin is back, this time with powerhouses Oscar Isaac and Carey Mulligan. Has he managed to keep the magic alive in spite of the popularity? [Live viewing log, major spoilers ahead]

Episode 1

In case your Netflix masthead did not tell you, the second season of the no-longer-a-hidden-gem show is out. Lee Sung Jin is back with another edition of my top TV show of 2023.

Oscar Isaac and Carey Mulligan reunite. It wasn't that long ago that I watched Inside Llewyn Davis and they carry the same dynamics — her being holier-than-thou but not as accomplished (plus a British accent in Beef), him talented but insufferable. As much as I am delighted that season 2 is a completely different storyline and not some painfully obvious cash-grab extension of season 1, it is hard not to compare it with season 1. The smarts and imagery of season 1 have been retained (the almost road crash was a nice little callback without being on the nose).

But — Beef season 1 had a lightness about it, the levity that comes from Steven Yeun feeling alive when Ali Wong chases him. The scene with Young Mazino and Ali Wong jumping on the bed after weeks of catfishing was poetic respite in the tense revenge. It might be harsh to judge one episode against an entire season but so far, the lack of whimsy is a downgrade. The only exception is the scene where Oscar Isaac has his golf club raised and the naive-in-love couple stare for a second before running away — the Edgar Wright / Buster Keaton effect. Anxiety only works when there are moments to show what peace looks like. Otherwise, it is just tedious.

One of the surprises was calling Mulligan/Crane's aesthetic sense as colonial. It does a lot — South Korea's own history with colonialism from Japan, and a commentary on how "traditional" aesthetics are viewed in a modern, multicultural world. It could sprawl into its own theme in the season. But there's too much already at play: commentaries and consequences of late-stage capitalism, complexities of old relationships, fragility of new ones and four main characters. While the jury is still out on the number of protagonists, they are all surprisingly fleshed out in a 40-minute episode.

With 8 episodes this season, can they explore all of them well or are they going to hint and leave?

PS: Oscar Isaac is delightful as always, even when he is an a-hole.

Episode 2

The melancholy over lack of whimsy in episode 1 is put at ease about 20 seconds into episode 2 — in a way that Beef knows how to do it best. It's people being uncharacteristically themselves in unexpected moments: like when two couples are negotiating blackmail. And ultimately, that's what makes Beef what it is — an unparalleled character study in modern television.

Lindsey (Carey Mulligan)'s don't-fuck-with-me instinct — the anecdote about getting a crowd to hate a guy who didn't like her, coercing the flirty tennis instructor to pay for her surgeries — she is a mercenary in floral prints.

Josh (Oscar Isaac) mourning his past self while navigating uncertain waters. Every time he tries to better himself, he fumbles (literally sometimes). The scene where he sells off his audio gear was just effectively heartbreaking without going into self-pity.

And the couple who are supposed to be their opposites — Cailee Spaeny and Charles Melton — are actually a mirror reflection across a time-gate (Can you tell I have been reading Ted Chiang?). Spaeny acts sweet but is the one who vouches for the blackmail (understandably so) from the beginning; Melton feeling inadequate in his career and resenting Spaeny for it. That's the inevitability of capitalism — the thesis of this season.

Beef season 2 sidesteps a lot of things I've come to hate in modern shows. It builds anxiety but doesn't make that the whole show (looking at you, The Bear). It doesn't resort to the modern editing strategy of not letting moments linger for transition shocks. And the screenplay is genuinely interesting — while season 1 was a deeper dive into two characters, this season is a web of people setting each other up for failure, sinking into the quicksand that is keeping up appearances, and the ultimate acceptance that this is a dog-eat-dog world to justify the corruption of their ideals.

Episode 3

Remember my initial reluctance about the number of protagonists, about 2 posts ago? That 4 might be too much, compared to the deeper insight into Steven Yeun and Ali Wong in Season 1? Well, I am being pushed even further - because this episode introduces yet another couple!

An older billionaire woman marries a younger surgeon man because she wanted someone who would have fewer problems than her. Ironically, she finds him getting her into trouble, has more health problems than her, and has to reassure him about not leaving.

While they are technically a different couple, belonging to a different social strata and age group, there are similarities in undertones: women who are pulling men up, them trying to keep their male counterpart in business with them because part of them feels like they are “drifting apart”, and the different ways they deal with the insecurities that come out in the men.

Storyline with 6 people is ambitious and a considerable step up in gamble from season 1 - so far it is pulled off well, but we have to wait and see what it costs, if any.

Lee Sung Jin also really leans into his Korean heritage in this episode, but in a way that he almost can't help himself. A criticism of the cosmetic surgery culture, the Kim Jung Un joke, and Ashley picking “China Bamboo House” as a welcome dinner spot for a Korean in America - none handled heavily but it is there and it is sharp.

The episodes continue to have their whimsy and it is handled so deftly. It does not veer into winking at the audience or gets conscious about taking itself seriously - instead you get a phone being passed around in an operating theatre with a dead person on the table.

But the true highlight of this episode? Ashley spirals, jumps out of a moving car, says she's “processing” while reciting the lyrics to 'Uptown Funk' and falls into a lake - all within about 10 seconds. Super cool.

Episode 4

The most tense episode of the show so far and there's lots to love: for example, the establishing of the broken window and the callback is incredibly well done.

But more importantly, it is an episode that shows everything wrong with the American healthcare system - all the nuances, losing none of the charm, pulling no punches. Luigi Mangione would be proud.

Episode 5

There's a scene in Episode 3, where Oscar Isaac and Carey Mulligan have checked themselves into a resort that is run by one of their friends, as a recon for their own passion project. Oscar Isaac is sitting in a chair on the hallway, under the amber light, on the left, while Carey Mulligan approaches his latitude, walking by the swimming pool and its fluorescent blue light on the right. She walks towards him, and Oscar Isaac asks “Are you okay?”. She says fine. Oscar Isaac walks away.

That foreshadowing comes to roost in Episode 5, with snobby friends, Hot Chip, weaponised therapy-talk and Burberry.

Burberry the dog is set up from the beginning (also what a name for a dog in a show about capitalism with a British protagonist). He is shown to want to be around them, and that he is important for the child-less couple that Josh and Lindsey are. This episode shows us what happens to everyone around him, in relation to him and each other, and it becomes the most gut-wrenching episode so far.

Lindsey continues to embody my read of her, as she takes on a coyote. There is something about jungle in the dark that Lee Sung Jin seems to love, as a metaphor and expositional device - it is not dissimilar to the crow conversation that happens in one of season 1's last episodes.

After watching too much violence porn on screen these days being sold as "essential for story", this episode shows how you can have your heart broken for a dog without ever seeing it actually suffer.

Episode 6

My least favourite episode this season (and probably across the franchise). While the men and women bandy against each other, the emotional range of the men and women is stark and uncomfortable. While Lindsey (Carey Mulligan) does recon for her divorce, take Ashley (Cailee Spaeny) under her wing in navigating the club, and have an emotionally resonant chat on a parking lot, Josh (Oscar Isaac) and Austin (Charles Melton) go on a ChatGPT-informed drug episode spouting what sounds meta on the surface but is of no substance about 90% of the way. (Which is a harder watch than when he has a hollow-but-honest conversation with his "friend" on a private jet). My dislike however does not come from the characters but from the screenplay which makes the episode seem so generic that it could have been in some other modern show. There were hardly any of the little surprise in emotional note that I've come to expect from Beef - or there was and it never landed. The one saving grace is the few minutes set in Korea and the ruthlessness of Chairwoman Park.

PS: Whoever came up with Austin's moustache in the character design spec knew what they were doing. It is a visual cue to his despicable personality.

Running verdict

A study of the effect of capitalism on human relationships, potentially at a cost of trying to rival 'White Lotus'. More to follow.
