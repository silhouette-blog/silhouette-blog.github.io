Beef, Season 2

Lee Sung Jin is back. Oscar Isaac and Carey Mulligan take over. The thesis this time is capitalism. It's still unparalleled.

Episode 2

The melancholy over lack of whimsy in episode 1 is put at ease about 20 seconds into episode 2 — in a way that Beef knows how to do it best. It's people being uncharacteristically themselves in unexpected moments: like when two couples are negotiating blackmail. And ultimately, that's what makes Beef a masterpiece — it is an unparalleled character study in modern television.

Lindsey (Carey Mulligan)'s don't-fuck-with-me instinct — the anecdote about getting a crowd to hate a guy who didn't like her, coercing the flirty tennis instructor to pay for her surgeries — she is a mercenary in floral prints.

Josh (Oscar Isaac) mourning his past self while navigating uncertain waters. Every time he tries to better himself, he fumbles (literally sometimes). The scene where he sells off his audio gear was just effectively heartbreaking without going into self-pity.

And the couple who are supposed to be their opposites — Cailee Spaeny and Charles Melton — are actually a mirror reflection across a time-gate. Spaeny acts sweet but is the one who vouches for the blackmail (understandably so) from the beginning; Melton feeling inadequate in his career and resenting Spaeny for it. That's the inevitability of capitalism — the thesis of this season.

Beef season 2 sidesteps a lot of things I've come to hate in modern shows. It builds anxiety but doesn't make that the whole show (looking at you, The Bear). It doesn't resort to the modern editing strategy of not letting moments linger for transition shocks. And the screenplay is genuinely interesting — while season 1 was a deeper dive into two characters, this season is a web of people setting each other up for failure, sinking into the quicksand that is keeping up appearances, and the ultimate acceptance that this is a dog-eat-dog world to justify the corruption of their ideals.

Episode 1

In case your Netflix masthead did not tell you, the second season of the no-longer-a-hidden-gem show is out. Lee Sung Jin is back with another edition of my top TV show of 2023.

Oscar Isaac and Carey Mulligan reunite. It wasn't that long ago that I watched Inside Llewyn Davis and they carry the same dynamics — her being holier-than-thou but not as accomplished (plus a British accent in Beef), him talented but insufferable. As much as I am delighted that season 2 is a completely different storyline and not some painfully obvious cash-grab extension of season 1, it is hard not to compare it with season 1. The smarts and imagery of season 1 have been retained (the almost road crash was a nice little callback without being on the nose).

But — Beef season 1 had a lightness about it, the levity that comes from Steven Yeun feeling alive when Ali Wong chases him. The scene with Young Mazino and Ali Wong jumping on the bed after weeks of catfishing was poetic respite in the tense revenge. It might be harsh to judge one episode against an entire season but so far, the lack of whimsy is a downgrade. The only exception is the scene where Oscar Isaac has his golf club raised and the naive-in-love couple stare for a second before running away — the Edgar Wright / Buster Keaton effect. Anxiety only works when there are moments to show what peace looks like. Otherwise, it is just tedious.

One of the surprises was calling Mulligan/Crane's aesthetic sense as colonial. It does a lot — South Korea's own history with colonialism from Japan, and a commentary on how "traditional" aesthetics are viewed in a modern, multicultural world. It could sprawl into its own theme in the season. But there's too much already at play: commentaries and consequences of late-stage capitalism, complexities of old relationships, fragility of new ones and four main characters. While the jury is still out on the number of protagonists, they are all surprisingly fleshed out in a 40-minute episode.

With 8 episodes this season, can they explore all of them well or are they going to hint and leave? PS: Oscar Isaac is delightful as always, even when he is an a-hole.

Running verdict

Still the best character study on television. More to follow.
